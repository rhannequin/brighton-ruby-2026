Time is a lie.

Brighton Ruby 2026 · @rhannequin

For most of human history,
the only clock was the sky.

Before we wrote any of this down, there was one clock: the sky. The Sun told you when to wake up, when to plant, when to harvest. The Moon told you when months were. The stars told you where north was.

Time was something you read, not something you stored.

October 4, 1582
→ ?

Let's start easy, we all know how to count days, right?

So, what day comes after October 4, 1582?

October 15, 1582

The next day was October 15, 1582, at least in Rome, and in the countries that adopted the Gregorian reform that week.

Ten days were simply skipped to realign the calendar with nature. Other countries did the same later, with different numbers of days, sometimes centuries later.

The October Revolution happened in…?

Ok, there was a trick, let's do something more obvious: what month did the October Revolution happen in?

November, 1917

in the Gregorian calendar

In Western Europe we witnessed the October Revolution in November, 1917 because the Gregorian calendar was only adopted in Russia in 1918, after the Russian Revolution.

December 31, 2016 23:59:59 → ?

Calendars are complicated. Let's try something simpler. Can you guess what's right after December 31, 2016 23:59:59?

December 31, 2016 23:59:60

in UTC

Yep, the minute actually lasted 61 seconds, because of a leap second added to keep our clocks in sync with the Earth's rotation.

One feature

When does the Sun rise tomorrow
in your city?

Before anything, let's pick one feature. A weather app, a prayer-time app, an astronomy app, it doesn't really matter. The whole feature is one question: when does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city?

Lie № 1

A day is 24 hours

Lie number one. Everybody knows a day has 24 hours of 60 minutes of 60 seconds. That's 86400 seconds.

Let me show this is wrong. 


          Time.zone = "Europe/London"
          t = Time.zone.local(2026, 10, 25, 0, 30, 0)
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 00:30:00 BST +01:00

Let's pick a time. Half past midnight in London, the morning of a day in October. I am going to add a day to it. 


          duration = 24 * 60 * 60
          # => 86400

          t + duration
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 23:30:00 GMT +00:00

We said 86400 seconds is exactly one day. But look closer at the result. Sunday, October 25. This is still on the same day. We added 24 hours and we did not reach Monday. 


          t + 24.hours
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 23:30:00 GMT +00:00

A lot of us know it, ActiveSupport monkey-patches Integer to add methods like hours or minutes.

24.hours reads nicer than 86400, but it does the same thing. Rails expands hours as elapsed seconds, there is no calendar math involved. We still don't reach Monday.

On October 25, 2026, in London, a day is 90,000 seconds long.

What's happening is that daylight saving time change will happen on October 25 in the UK. The clocks here go back at 2 AM. The hour between 1 and 2 happens twice. So that day is 25 hours long. 90,000 seconds.

Truth № 1 

          t + 1.day
          # => Mon, 26 Oct 2026 00:30:00 GMT +00:00

Both 24.hours and 1.day are ActiveSupport::Duration objects. But a duration is an abstract notion on its own, you also need to know its unit.

ActiveSupport patches the + method of the Time class when it receives a Duration. With hours, it adds elapsed seconds. With days, it moves the calendar.

Use the object that means what you want.

One of the main things to remember from the few minutes we have together is to use the object that means what you want. Semantic matters.

For our sunrise feature, this means: tomorrow is not 86400 seconds from now, it's one day from now. 


          jan_31 = Time.zone.local(2026, 1, 31)

          jan_31 + 1.month   # => Sat, 28 Feb 2026
          jan_31 + 2.months  # => Tue, 31 Mar 2026
          jan_31 + 30.days   # => Mon, 02 Mar 2026

Let's see just a few examples before we move on.

+ 1.month is aware of the end of the month so it lands on the 28th and not February 31.

+ 2.months anchors back to the original day, which is why it lands on March 31, not on the 28th plus a month.

And + 30.days just counts days, ignoring every month boundary.

Lie № 2

Time zones are geography

Lie number two. Time zones do look like geography. You take a globe, you draw 24 vertical lines, and you call each strip a zone.

But as you know, the real story is more complicated.

Before 1840, every town set its clock by the Sun.

Bristol · 11:51
Brighton · 11:59
London · 12:00
Norwich · 12:05

Before 1840, every town set its own clock by the Sun. Noon was when the Sun was directly overhead, and at a given instant the Sun is in a slightly different place seen from Bristol than from Norwich.

So when London read 12:00, Bristol read 11:51. Brighton read 11:59. Norwich read 12:05. Local mean solar time.

And it worked, because nobody could travel faster than the Sun moved.

Then we invented trains.

1847 · one railway time.

Then we invented trains. Take a two-hour journey from London to Bristol. From one station to the other, your clock would be out of sync with the local time by nine minutes.

In 1847, British railways agreed to use Greenwich Mean Time at every station, wherever it sat. Local solar time was gone for everyone except Greenwich, and other countries followed.

Then politics drew the lines.

Then governments started bending the lines. China is wide enough for five zones but uses one. India is offset by half an hour. Nepal by forty-five minutes. Spain sits on the same meridian as Britain but reads its clocks an hour ahead, because Franco aligned with Berlin in 1940 and nobody moved it back.

Time zones look like geography, but they're really political decisions.

We traded astronomical accuracy
for human convenience.

That's the trade we made. The Sun stopped telling us the time, then the trains did, then governments did. We decided it was worth it, because the alternative was unreadable timetables and meetings that started at different moments for different people.

The IANA database is the receipt.

600+ zones · updated several times a year

Back to time zones.

We store all the crazy timezone shenanigans in the IANA time zone database. More than 600 zones, updated several times a year, because somewhere in the world a government keeps deciding to do something different with their clocks.

In Ruby it's available through the tzinfo gem. 


          tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("America/Asuncion")
          tz.utc_to_local(Time.utc(2025, 7, 1, 12, 0, 0))

2025-07-01 08:00:00  old tzinfo

2025-07-01 09:00:00  recent tzinfo

But as everything in computing, you need to keep your dependencies up-to-date.

Paraguay observed DST until late 2024, then abolished it in 2025. With the same code, you can have two different answers, depending on whether your tzinfo gem is recent enough to know about the change.

If it is out of date, your app is following time zone rules that no longer apply.

Truth № 2

Store IANA names.
Keep tzinfo current.

Rails has its own way to refer to time zones and sometimes uses more friendly names, but they can change and they don't work outside of Rails. So, if you need to, it's better to store the IANA name of the zone.

And you might need to. For our sunrise feature, this is where latitude and longitude stop being enough. Because local time is not tied to your coordinates on Earth but in which administration you are.

And of course, keep tzinfo current, because the database keeps changing.

Lie № 3

Time.now gives current time

Lie number three. Time.now. The name says it gives you the current time, and it does, but the current time according to the machine, not according to your app. 


            config.time_zone = "UTC"

            Time.now              # => 00:25:58 +0100
            Time.current          # => 23:25:58 UTC

            Time.now.to_date      # => 2026-06-25
            Time.current.to_date  # => 2026-06-24

My configuration says UTC, but Time.now ignores it. Time.now reads the system clock and the system zone, it doesn't look at your application config.

We might argue that it doesn't matter because both objects represent the same instant, from different points of view. It's true but in lots of cases we don't actually need the instant alone, we need it in a context.

So when you ask the date on each of these objects, now you have different ones.

And that's the first thing to keep in mind for our feature. Depending on how we build the time object, we could have the sunrise time for the wrong day.

Truth № 3

Ruby → Time.now.utc

Rails → Time.current

In a Rails app, you should prefer Time.current as it matches the zone you configured. Or you can use Time.now.utc in plain Ruby and default to the universal time.

Lie № 4

Time.parse is reliable

Lie number four. Time.parse. You give it a string and you get a time back. That sounds reliable, but look at what it does with this string. 


            Time.parse("2026-05-29 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-05-29 09:00:00 +0200

There is no +2 time zone component in the input. It came from my laptop. If I deploy this code to a server in another zone, it returns a different value for the same string. But once again we have a nice workaround in ActiveSupport.

Truth № 4 

            Time.zone = "UTC"

            Time.zone.parse("2026-05-29 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-05-29 09:00:00 UTC

The important part is who owns the fallback. Time.parse falls back to whatever the host machine is set to. Time.zone.parse falls back to the zone your application declared. It still makes an assumption, but this time it's one you control.

Lie № 5

1:30 AM happens once

Lie number five. 1:30 in the morning happens once a day, just like every other minute. Except there's a day where it happens twice, and a day where it doesn't happen at all.

Daylight saving time started as an industrial scheduling experiment. Nowadays most countries that observe it move the clocks by one hour, twice a year. And each of those moves can cause problems in your code. 


            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("Europe/London")

            tz.local_time(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)

I'm going to ask the London zone for a Time at 1:30 AM on March 29. 


            tz.local_time(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)
            # => TZInfo::PeriodNotFound

That time never happened in London. When the clocks spring forward, the wall clock jumps straight over it, so there is no London instant to return. If you store it you've stored something that was never real. 


            tz.local_time(2026, 10, 25, 1, 30, 0)
            # => TZInfo::AmbiguousTime

Here is a second one, 6 months later.

When the clocks go back, the same wall-clock time happens twice, so the zone won't pick one for you. It asks which one you meant. 


            Time.zone = "Europe/London"

            Time.zone.parse("2026-03-29 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 29 Mar 2026 02:30:00 BST +01:00

            Time.zone.parse("2026-10-25 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 01:30:00 BST +01:00

And it gets worse. If you use Time.zone.parse, it picks one of the two times for you, silently, without raising anything. You just have to hope it picked the one you meant.

Truth № 5

Twice a year, a clock time is missing or doubled.

Rare, but let it raise, don't let it guess.

I'll be honest, this is an edge case. Most apps will never hit it, and you don't need to defend every call against it. But it's real, it happens twice a year, and Time.zone.parse will quietly pick a side for you.

Our feature is mostly safe here, unless we set a notification based on a few hours before sunrise, but that would be very unlucky.

The only thing I would say is it's probably better to rely on the method local_time from TZInfo. At least it will loudly raise if something goes wrong instead of providing wrong data silently.

Lie № 6

JSON and the database preserve your time

Lie number six. You did the work in Ruby. You used Time.zone, you handled DST, your tests pass. Now you need to send it somewhere, a JSON response or store it in a database column. That's where you can lose what you just built. 


            Time.zone = "UTC"

            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

At this point you might feel safe. You're using zone, good for you, the string includes the offset, and this date is nowhere near the tricky ones we've seen so far. 


            Time.zone = "UTC"

            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

            # => "2026-06-25T08:00:00.000Z"

And yes, nothing broke, nothing raised, you got a valid JSON string for a valid time.

You can see we moved from 9 o'clock to 8 o'clock, but it's just the UTC conversion.

Actually, the UTC conversion just made you lose the only piece of context you had. Now there's no way to remember the time zone offset.

We better hope the app that consumes our sunrise time feature remembers the time zone because the information is lost.

Truth № 6

Outside Ruby objects, don't forget the zone.

Once you leave Ruby for serialization or storage, remember the zone won't follow you. If you need it, make sure to include it.

It comes down to what you're carrying. If it's an instant, a real moment in time, then UTC is enough and the zone is just for display. But if you're carrying an intent, a local time that's meant to stay local, like an alarm at 7am, then the zone is part of the meaning and you have to keep it.

Database warning

PostgreSQL: timestamptz

MySQL: datetime

SQLite: no datetime type

If you need to store the time and keep the zone, you have to store them separately.

PostgreSQL's timestamptz stores an instant, just the moment, normalised to UTC. The name is misleading, nothing about the zone survives the round-trip.

MySQL has two types: datetime stores calendar fields with no zone at all, and timestamp converts through the session zone but only covers a limited range.

SQLite has no datetime type at all, so you store a string or an integer and handle the zone yourself.

If the zone matters, store it next to the instant, with the IANA name in its own column.

Lie № 7

Noon is when the Sun is highest

Lie number seven, and this one is just a little bonus to get some wonder. When the clock strikes twelve, you look up, and the Sun is at its highest point. That's the definition of noon. But on most days, it isn't true.

In London, solar noon happens at:

Feb 11 · 12:14
Nov 3 · 11:44

On February 11, in London, the Sun reaches its highest point at 12:14. On November 3, it gets there at 11:44. That's half an hour apart, depending only on the day of the year.

This is called the equation of time and it exists because of the Earth's orbit and axis. In short it means the Sun runs a bit fast for part of the year and a bit slow for the rest.

Now, this is probably not something that would bother us programmers but I still wanted to mention it. We use a mean solar time in two manners: to define the length of a day, and to define the hours on the clock.

One year of solar noons.

If you photograph the Sun at the same clock time every day for a year, you don't get a single dot, you get this: a figure-8 called an analemma. The vertical axis is the tilt of the Earth, the horizontal axis is the equation of time. Every dot is a day the clock and the Sun disagreed.

Truth № 7

The clock is not the Sun.

If you are computing sunrise, sunset, prayer times, or the position of anything in the sky, you can't get that from the clock. You have to compute it from the Sun itself.

That's what our sunrise feature does. The clock only labels the answer once we have it.

This is not code but I think it's good to know that a lot of the weird stuff we have to deal with related to time is just a reflection of nature's chaotic and beautiful side.

Toolkit

What to remember.

Alright, this was it.

Seven lies is a lot to remember. So here is what to actually keep.

Back to the sunrise.

Remember our one feature: when does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city? We've spent seven lies watching that question break. Let's actually build it now.

Don't 

          t.datetime :next_sunrise_at

Don't store the sunrise. Don't store the time at all. The user can move countries, experience DST and the sunrise time would be wrong.

In some cases, you'll want to store the inputs that produce a result, rather than the result itself.

Do 

          t.decimal :latitude
          t.decimal :longitude
          t.string  :time_zone

Store the intent: where the user is, and which zone they read time in. Then compute the sunrise when you actually need it. A latitude and longitude don't change when DST does.

Store the question.

Compute the answer.

If you need to set a recurring reminder or send a delayed notification, it's better to store the parameters and compute the result when you actually need it.

Testing 

          travel_to Time.utc(2026, 10, 25, 0, 30) do
            # A 90,000-second day in London.
          end

Test the difficult dates your clock can actually represent: October 25, March 29, February 29, midnight on the day Paraguay abolished daylight saving.

Tests are also meant to cover edge cases.

Showing time to humans

10:23 +03:00

10:23 AST

10:23 Asia/Riyadh

10:23 Asia/Riyadh (UTC+3)

Three ways to show a time. The UTC offset is accurate and is enough most of the time, just keep in mind you don't have the time zone context if you want to reuse it. Acronyms are short, but AST is both Arabia Standard Time and Atlantic Standard Time, and those are seven hours apart. IANA names are unambiguous, but not much help if you don't recognise the zone.

If you want absolute precision, then combine them. The zone name tells you which zone it is, the offset lets you do the maths. It isn't always needed, but if your app crosses zones, this is a very effective format, especially for users who never see DST or never read offsets.

The checklist
Instant? Store a UTC instant
Zone matters? Store the IANA name separately
Current time? Time.current
Today? Date.current
Calendar math? 1.day, advance, change
Machine input? Require ISO 8601 with an offset
Trusted app-zone string? Time.zone.parse
User wall time? tz.local_time; handle gaps & overlaps
Schedule? Store intent. Compute the instant.
Test? travel_to an evil date

I guess if there's one slide to keep with you, it would be this one. If you're an experienced developer you probably already use most of these techniques because they became best practices. But I wanted to remind why it's important to follow them and what could happen otherwise.

Dates don't always follow.

Time zones change under you.

Even the Sun and the Moon don't always rise.

Now you know, a day is not always 24 hours, dates don't always follow each other, and time zones don't sit still.

Weirdly enough, even the Sun and the Moon don't always rise. If you live in the North of Norway, there are days without sunrise. And because the Moon's rising drifts about fifty minutes every day, there are some local 24-hour windows that just don't contain a moonrise.

Don't trust your instincts about time.

Time was never going to be simple.

We started with a calendar that lost ten days in 1582. We ended in a polar winter in Norway where the Sun doesn't rise for days and nil is the only honest answer.
