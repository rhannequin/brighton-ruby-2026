Time is a lie.

Brighton Ruby 2026 · @rhannequin

For most of human history,
the only clock was the sky.

The sky has been our clock for most of our history. The Sun told us when to wake up, when to plant, when to harvest. It gave us the concept of day-and-night, and the seasons, while we imagined 12 months from the Moon cycles.

Time was real and true. And then, it started to lie.

Sir Isaac Newton

25 December 1642

4 January 1643

Let's start with a bit of history.

Sir Isaac Newton was born in England, on 25 December 1642. But did you know this lucky person had another birth date?

Both dates are correct, the first is in the Julian calendar, invented many centuries before and still used in Great Britain at the time.

The second is in the Gregorian calendar, introduced by Pope Gregory 13 in 1582. This calendar fixed a growing gap of 10 days with nature.

Same day, but different dates. I can't imagine how historians deal with this. Today, we are going to uncover a few traps we can fall into when we deal with time in our code.

One reminder

“Remind me at 7am,
wherever I am.”

Alright.

Let's keep one thing in mind for the whole talk. Just a reminder from a user: remind me at 7 in the morning, wherever I am.

You've probably built something like this. A notification, a recurring email, an alarm. And we're going to watch six different lies about time get between you and sending that one notification.

Lie № 1

A day is 24 hours

Lie number one. Everybody knows a day has 24 hours of 60 minutes of 60 seconds. That's 86400 seconds.

Let me show this is wrong.


          Time.zone = "Europe/London"
          t = Time.zone.local(2026, 10, 25, 0, 30, 0)
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 00:30:00 BST +01:00

Let's pick a time. Half past midnight in London, the morning of a day in October. I am going to add a day to it. 


          duration = 24 * 60 * 60
          # => 86400

          t + duration
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 23:30:00 GMT +00:00

We said 86400 seconds is exactly one day. But look closer at the result. Sunday, October 25. This is still on the same day. We added 24 hours and we did not reach Monday. 


          t + 24.hours
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 23:30:00 GMT +00:00

So, let's just use Rails. A lot of us know it, ActiveSupport monkey-patches the Integer class to add methods like hours or minutes.

This way we can write 24 dot hours , which reads nicer than the arbitrary 86400 number. But it does the same thing. Rails expands the hours method as elapsed seconds, so there is no calendar math involved. And we still don't reach Monday.

On October 25, 2026, in London, a day is 90,000 seconds long.

What's happening is that daylight-saving-time-change will happen exactly on October 25 in the UK. The clocks here go back at 2 AM. The hour between 1 and 2 happens twice. So that day is 25 hours long, or 90,000 seconds.

Truth № 1 

          t + 1.day
          # => Mon, 26 Oct 2026 00:30:00 GMT +00:00

Both 24.hours and 1.day are ActiveSupport::Duration objects, but they don't do the same thing. With the hours method, the addition adds a strict duration in seconds. With the days method, it moves the calendar.

Use the object that means what you want.

One of the main things to remember from the few minutes we have together is to use the object that means what you want. Semantics matter.

For our reminder, this means tomorrow's 7am is one day from now, not 86400 seconds from now. 


          jan_31 = Time.zone.local(2026, 1, 31)

          jan_31 + 1.month   # => Sat, 28 Feb 2026
          jan_31 + 2.months  # => Tue, 31 Mar 2026
          jan_31 + 30.days   # => Mon, 02 Mar 2026

Let's see a few examples. + 1 dot month knows the month ends on the 28th, so it lands there, not on February 31.
In the same way, + 2 dot months reaches the end of March, even if February and March don't have the same number of days.

But + 30 dot days just counts days, ignoring the month boundary entirely, because this duration has a different meaning.

Again, this is all about semantics.

Lie № 2

Time zones are geography

Lie number two. You know time zones, and they do look like geography. You take a globe, you draw 24 vertical lines, and you call each strip a zone.

Easy.

Before 1840, every town set its clock by the Sun.

Bristol · 11:50
Brighton · 11:59
London · 12:00
Norwich · 12:05

Before 1840, every town had its own clock related to the Sun. Noon was when the Sun reached its highest point in the sky, as seen from there.
But at a given instant, the Sun is seen in a different place everywhere on Earth.

So when London reads 12:00, Bristol reads 11:50. Brighton reads 11:59. And Norwich reads 12:05. This is called local solar time.

And it worked, because nobody could travel faster than the Sun moved.

Then we invented trains.

1847 · one railway time.

Then we invented trains. At the end of a journey from Bristol to London, your clock would be nine minutes out of sync with the local time in London.

So in 1847, British railways standardised on the Greenwich Mean Time, or GMT, and train stations followed a single conventional time.

And then, other entire countries followed.

Then politics drew the lines.

Then governments started bending the lines. As you can see, China is wide enough for five zones but uses only one. India is offset with UTC by half an hour. Nepal by forty-five minutes. And if you look at Spain, it sits on the same meridian as Britain but it reads its clocks an hour ahead, because they shifted to Central European Time during the Franco era and never moved back.

Time zones look like geography, but they're really political decisions.

We traded astronomical accuracy
for human convenience.

That's the trade we made. The Sun stopped telling us the time when the trains did, and then governments did. We decided it was worth it, because the alternative was unreadable timetables back then. And today, the coordination between people across the world would be unbearable.

The IANA database is the receipt.

600+ zones · updated several times a year

Back to time zones.

We store all the crazy timezone shenanigans in the IANA time zone database. More than 600 zones, updated several times a year, because somewhere in the world a government keeps deciding to do something different.

In Ruby it's available through the tzinfo gem.


            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("America/Asuncion")
            tz.utc_to_local(Time.utc(2025, 7, 1, 12, 0, 0))
2025-07-01 08:00:00 old tzinfo 2025-07-01 09:00:00 recent tzinfo

But as everything in computing, you need to keep your dependencies up-to-date.

Did you know that Paraguay observed DST until late 2024, and then abolished it in 2025?

With the same code, you can have two different answers, depending on whether your tzinfo gem is recent enough to know about the time zone change.

If it is out of date, your app is following time zone rules that no longer apply.

Truth № 2

Store IANA names.
Keep tzinfo current.

You might need to store the time zone of your users. Not the one that comes from Rails it has its own way to refer to time zones with sometimes more friendly names, but they can change and they don't work outside of Rails. So, if you need to, it's better to store the IANA name of the zone.

The name itself isn't frozen either. IANA keeps the old names working through backward-compatibility links, so a value you stored years ago keeps resolving.

And we do need to store it. Our reminder fires at 7am wherever the user is, so we have to know which zone they read 7am in. Local time isn't tied to your coordinates on Earth, it's tied to which administration you're under.

Lie № 3

Time is the same everywhere it runs

Lie number three. There are two methods from the class Time that we reach for constantly: Time dot now , to get the current time, and Time dot parse , to read a string. From their names, both look like they would be very predictable and reliable. 


            config.time_zone = "UTC"

            Time.now              # => 01:25:58 +0200
            Time.current          # => 23:25:58 UTC

            Time.now.to_date      # => 2026-06-25
            Time.current.to_date  # => 2026-06-24

My configuration says UTC, but Time.now ignores it. Time.now reads the system clock and the system zone, it doesn't look at your application config.

So when using the method to_date on them to get the associated date, you get different ones, coming from the same instant. 


            Time.parse("2026-07-24 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-07-24 09:00:00 +0200

Here as well, there is no +2 time zone component in the input. It came from my laptop. If I deploy this code to a server in another zone, it returns a different value for the same string.

But hopefully, once again we have a nice workaround in ActiveSupport.

Truth № 3

Time.now → Time.current

Time.parse → Time.zone.parse

Plain Ruby → Time.now.utc

It's the same fix for both. Time.current and Time.zone.parse will fall back to the zone your application declared.

And that's it for this one, just remember to use Time.current and zone.

If you're just with standard Ruby, Time.now.utc is a great default too.

Lie № 4

1:30 AM happens once

Lie number four. We talked about daylight saving time already. But there's one more side effect to it that your code is not going to like.

Most countries that observe daylight saving move the clocks by one hour, twice a year. We've already noticed that when the clocks go back, the day is 25 hours long. Let's see what happens when they move forward. 


            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("Europe/London")

            tz.local_time(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)

I'm going to ask the London zone for a Time at 1:30 AM on March 29. 


            tz.local_time(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)
            # => TZInfo::PeriodNotFound

That time never happened in London. When the time move forward, the clock jumps straight over it, so there is no London instant to return. If you have such a time-and-zone stored somewhere, then you've stored something that never existed. And the code raises. 


            tz.local_time(2026, 10, 25, 1, 30, 0)
            # => TZInfo::AmbiguousTime

Here is a second one, 6 months later.

When the clocks go back, the same wall-clock time happens twice, so the zone won't pick one for you. It asks which one you meant by raising another exception. 


            Time.zone = "Europe/London"

            Time.zone.parse("2026-03-29 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 29 Mar 2026 02:30:00 BST +01:00

            Time.zone.parse("2026-10-25 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 01:30:00 BST +01:00

What if we don't use TZinfo then?

Here are the same two inputs through Time.zone.parse. The forward case that didn't exist, and the back case that happened twice. Rails gives you a default result for both, without raising, and without telling you it had to choose.

TZInfo raises.

Time.zone.parse resolves silently.

The two APIs handle this differently. TZInfo will raise when facing an issue, while Time dot zone dot parse takes the same input, picks one, and returns it with no warning.

Truth № 4

Twice a year, a clock time is missing or doubled.

This is an edge case. Most apps will never hit it, and you don't need to defend every call against it. But it's real, and it happens twice a year.

Our reminder mostly avoids this, unless the user picks a time right in the gap, like 1:30 in the morning. That's rare, but it can happen.

When you do need to handle a user's wall-clock time, I suggest using TZInfo directly to avoid silent surprises.

Lie № 5

JSON and databases preserve your time

Lie number five. You did the work in Ruby. You used Time dot zone , you handled DST, all your tests pass. Now you need to send it somewhere, a JSON response or store it in a database column. That's where you can lose what you just built. 


            Time.zone = "UTC"

            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

At this point you might feel safe. You didn't forget to use the zone, good for you, the string includes the offset, and this date is nowhere near the tricky ones we've seen so far. 


            Time.zone = "UTC"

            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

            # => "2026-06-25T08:00:00.000Z"

And yes, nothing broke, nothing raised, you got a valid JSON string for a valid time.

We moved from 9 o'clock to 8 o'clock, but that's just the UTC conversion.

What we actually lost is the offset. The string had +01:00, and the JSON doesn't. So we lost some of the context. And even this context was not complete because UTC+1 can be London in summer or it can be Lagos, it is just an offset and not a full time zone.

If the consumer needs the user's civil time, the IANA name has to travel separately, in its own field.

Truth № 5

Outside Ruby objects, don't forget the zone.

Once you leave Ruby for serialization or storage, remember the zone won't follow you. If you need it, make sure to include it.

It comes down to what you're carrying. If it's an instant, a real moment in time, then UTC is enough and the zone is just for display. But if you're carrying an intent, a local time that's meant to stay local, like our alarm at 7am, then the zone is part of the meaning, and you have to keep it.

No column type keeps your zone

PostgreSQL timestamptz · stored as UTC

MySQL DATETIME · ignores the zone

SQLite · no datetime type at all

Most of the storage systems we use won't make it easy for us.

The biggest trap is PostgreSQL's timestamptz. The name reads like it carries a zone, but it doesn't. The value gets normalised to UTC on the way in, and comes back in the system's time zone.

MySQL has DATETIME that ignores zones entirely.

And SQLite doesn't even have a datetime type.

If the zone matters to your domain, you need to store it next to the instant, with the IANA name in its own column.

Lie № 6

Noon is when the Sun is highest

Lie number six. Until now our reminder fired at 7am, a number the user gave us. But some reminders don't work that way. What if what the user really wants is to be notified at mid-day? Easy, that's noon, right? No, the user meant mid-day, when the Sun reaches its highest point in the sky. And that's almost never at 12 o'clock.

In London, solar noon happens at:

Feb 11 · 12:14
Nov 3 · 11:44

On February 11, in London, the Sun reaches its highest point at 12:14. On November 3, it gets there at 11:44. That's half an hour apart, depending only on the day of the year.

This is called the equation of time and it exists because the Earth's orbit is not a perfect circle. In short it means the Sun runs a bit fast for part of the year and a bit slow for the rest.

Clock time is an average, that's the "mean" in "mean solar time". It lines up close enough with the real Sun, but it's a human construction, not what actually happening up there.

One year of solar noons.

If you photograph the Sun at the same clock time every day for a year, you don't get a single dot, you get this: a figure-8 called an analemma. All these dots show the many times the clock and the Sun disagreed. And the Sun is exactly where it should be on every one of them. It's the clock that's averaging, pretending time is regular.

I know this is not code but I think it's good to know that a lot of the weird stuff we have to deal with related to time is just a reflection of nature's chaotic and beautiful side.

Back to the reminder.

Notify the user at 7am, wherever they are. We've spent six lies watching that one notification possibly break. Let's try to build it correctly.

Not the source of truth 

          t.datetime :next_reminder_at

The trap is making the computed time the thing you go-back-to when you need the answer. If the user moves country or DST shifts, then the value you stored is wrong, with nothing telling you it is.

It's not wrong to store a computed time but it is important to remember the intention.

Source of truth 

          t.time   :remind_at
          t.string :time_zone

The truth is the inputs that produce the answer. The local time the user asked for, and which zone they read it in. We compute the next fire time when we actually need it.

Store the question.

Compute the answer.

For a recurring reminder or a delayed notification, store the parameters and compute the result when you need it.

If the user's context changes, it means the parameters are updated, and the computed time will stay relevant.

Compute 

          def next_reminder_for(remind_at, zone)
            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get(zone)
            tomorrow = tz.utc_to_local(Time.now.utc).to_date + 1

            tz.local_time(
              tomorrow.year, tomorrow.month, tomorrow.day,
              remind_at.hour, remind_at.min, 0
            )
          end

Let's see how it could look like.

We get the zone from its IANA name.

Time dot now dot utc is the current moment. The zone turns it into a local date, and we add one calendar day. That's the accurate definition of tomorrow, where the user lives.

Then local_time method builds the instant the user asked for, 7am of their time.

If we want to be super cautious, we could also add some error handling for the DST edge cases, because that's where it could raise with ambiguous times.

And that's just it.

Showing time to humans

10:23 +03:00

10:23 AST

10:23 Asia/Riyadh

10:23 Asia/Riyadh (UTC+3)

One more thing, if you want to display a time, there are multiple ways. The UTC offset does the job most of the time, but it can be ambiguous. Time zone acronyms are short, but AST is both Arabia Standard Time and Atlantic Standard Time, and those are seven hours apart. IANA names are absolute, but who actually remembers the zone names?

If you want absolute precision, then you can combine them. The zone name tells you which zone it is, the offset lets you do the maths. I know it looks overkill, but if your app crosses zones, or if like me you like accuracy and precision, this is a very effective format.

Ask first

Is this an instant?

Is this a local time?

Is this a recurring intention?

Who owns the zone?

When dealing with time, I encourage you to ask yourself the right questions first. Name what you have before you pick a method. The same datetime column can mean an instant, a clock time, or a recurring intention, and each one wants a different tool.

The checklist
Now Time.current
Today Date.current
Calendar math 1.day, advance, change
Machine input ISO 8601 with an offset
Trusted app-zone string Time.zone.parse
User wall time tz.local_time
Store the zone IANA name, its own column
Schedule Store intent. Compute the instant.
Test travel_to an evil date
