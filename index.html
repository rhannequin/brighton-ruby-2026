Time is a lie.

Brighton Ruby 2026 · @rhannequin

For most of human history,
the only clock was the sky.

Before we wrote any of this down, there was one clock: the sky. The Sun told you when to wake up, when to plant, when to harvest. The Moon told you when months were. The stars told you where North was.

Time was something you read, not something you stored.

We invented railways. We invented Ruby.

The sky did not change.

Our code is still trying to catch up.

Then we invented railways, and trains needed schedules, and schedules needed a shared clock. We invented standard time. We invented time zones. We invented Unix timestamps, ISO 8601, Ruby, ActiveSupport.

The sky did not change. The Earth still wobbles, the Moon still slows us down, the year is still not a whole number of days. Our code is still trying to catch up.

Let me show you what trying to catch up looks like.

October 4, 1582, Rome → ?

Let's start easy, we all know how to count days, right?

So, what day comes after October 4, 1582, in Rome?

October 15, 1582

The next day was October 15, 1582 — in Rome, and in the countries that adopted the Gregorian reform that week.

Ten days were simply skipped to realign the calendar with nature. Other countries did the same later, with different numbers of days, sometimes centuries later.

The October Revolution happened in…?

Ok, there was a trick, let's do something more obvious: what month did the October Revolution happen in?

November, 1917

in the Gregorian Calendar

In Western Europe we witnessed the October Revolution in November, 1917 because the Gregorian calendar was only adopted in Russia in 1918, after the Russian Revolution.

December 31, 2016 23:59:59 → ?

Calendars are complicated, let's do something more simple. Can you guess what's right after December 31, 2016 23:59:59?

December 31, 2016 23:59:60

in UTC

Yep, the minute actually lasted 61 seconds, because of a leap second added to keep our clocks in sync with the Earth's rotation.

One feature

When does the Sun rise tomorrow
in your city?

Mostly Ruby. Occasionally Rails-shaped Ruby.

Before we look at any lie, let's pick a feature. One feature. A weather app, a prayer-time app, an astronomy app, doesn't matter. The whole feature is one question: when does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city?

Sounds simple. A time of day, computed from a location. We are going to watch this question get answered wrong in seven different ways.

One quick caveat before we start: this is Brighton Ruby, but most of what I'll show you leans on ActiveSupport. 1.day, Time.current, Time.zone, travel_to — all Rails. Vanilla Ruby's Time doesn't have advance or change. If you're not on Rails, the lessons still apply, but the method names won't.

Lie № 1

A day is 24 hours

Lie number one. And this one is bold. Everybody knows a day has 24 hours. So 24 times 3600, that's 86,400 seconds. It is the kind of fact you don't even check. You just use it.

Let me show you a morning that does not have 24 hours. 


          Time.zone = "Europe/London"
          t = Time.zone.parse("2026-10-25 00:30:00")
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 00:30:00 BST +01:00

Let's pick a time. Half past midnight in London, the morning of October 25, 2026. Looks innocent. I am going to add a day to it. The simplest thing a developer can do with a time. 


          duration = 24 * 60 * 60
          # => 86400

          t + duration
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 23:30:00 GMT +00:00

86,400 seconds. That's a day. Look at the result. Sunday, October 25. We are still on the same day. We added 24 hours and we did not reach Monday.

On October 25, 2026, in London, a day is 90,000 seconds long.

On October 25, 2026, the clocks in the UK go back at 2 AM. The hour between 1 and 2 happens twice. So that day is 25 hours long. 90,000 seconds.

Your scheduling code doesn't know that. + 86400 certainly doesn't know that.

Truth № 1 

          t + 1.day
          # => Mon, 26 Oct 2026 00:30:00 GMT +00:00

1.day is not a number. It is an ActiveSupport::Duration, a value object that knows what it means.

ActiveSupport patches Time#+ to call advance when it sees a Duration, instead of just adding seconds. The diff looks tiny, but the type changed: we stopped adding a number and started adding a calendar day. The math now knows about the calendar.

Use the object that means what you want.

This is the rule. The whole talk, in one sentence. 1.day is just the start — it generalises to everything else we are going to see.

For our sunrise feature, this means: tomorrow is not now + 86400. We're going to keep finding "not quite" sentences like that one. 


          jan_31 = Time.zone.local(2026, 1, 31)

          jan_31 + 1.month   # => Sat, 28 Feb 2026
          jan_31 + 2.months  # => Tue, 31 Mar 2026
          jan_31 + 30.days   # => Mon, 02 Mar 2026

Three operations, three different answers. + 1.month is end-of-month aware — February 31 does not exist, so it lands on the 28th. + 2.months anchors back to the original day, which is why it lands on March 31, not on the 28th plus a month. And + 30.days just counts days, ignoring every month boundary.

And while we're here: jan_31 + 2.months is not the same idea as jan_31 + 1.month + 1.month. The first anchors back to the 31st and lands on March 31. The second lands on February 28, then advances one more month from there — March 28. Two different answers, same-looking expression.

Under the hood, 1.month calls advance — same lesson, different unit. Each one has its own meaning. Pick the one that matches what you actually want.

Same day, different hour


          Time.current.change(
            hour: 9, min: 0, sec: 0
          )

change rewrites parts of a time without touching the rest. Useful when you want "today, but at 9 AM in the user's zone". We are going to see this method again later, in the scheduling example.

Lie № 2

Time zones are geography

Lie number two. Time zones look like geography. You take a globe, you draw 24 vertical lines, you call each strip a zone. That is the version we teach children. The real one is messier.

Before 1840, every town set its clock by the Sun.

Penzance · 11:38
Bristol · 11:51
London · 12:00
Norwich · 12:05

Before 1840, every town set its own clock by the Sun. Noon was when the Sun was directly overhead, and the Sun is in a slightly different place over Penzance than over Norwich. So when London read 12:00, Penzance read 11:38. Bristol read 11:51. Norwich read 12:05.

These were not approximations. These were the times. Local mean solar time. And it was fine — because nobody could move faster than the Sun did.

Then we invented trains.

1847 · one railway time.

Then we invented trains. A two-hour journey from London to Bristol now meant arriving in another time. Railway timetables became unreadable. Stations posted two clocks, and Bristol cathedral kept two minute hands — one for Bristol time, one for railway time.

In 1847, British railways agreed to use Greenwich Mean Time at every station, regardless of where it sat. Local solar time died for everyone except Greenwich. Other countries followed.

Then politics drew the lines.

Then governments started bending the lines. China is one zone wide enough to be five. India is offset by half an hour. Nepal by forty-five minutes. Spain sits on the same meridian as Britain but reads its clocks an hour ahead, because Franco aligned with Berlin in 1940 and nobody ever moved it back.

Time zones look like geography. They are political maps with a sundial pinned to them.

We traded astronomical accuracy
for human convenience.

That's the deal. The Sun stopped telling us the time. The trains did. Then the politicians did. We agreed it was worth it, because the alternative was unreadable timetables and meetings that started at different moments for different attendees.

The receipt for that trade is a database.

The IANA database is the receipt.

600+ zones · updated several times a year

The IANA time zone database. More than 600 zones. Updated several times a year — because somewhere in the world, a government has just decided to do something different with their clocks.

You don't read this database directly. You pick it up with bundle update tzinfo. That command is not a chore — it is you collecting the latest receipt. 


          tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("America/Asuncion")
          tz.utc_to_local(Time.utc(2025, 7, 1, 12, 0, 0))

2025-07-01 08:00:00  old tzinfo

2025-07-01 09:00:00  recent tzinfo

Paraguay observed DST until late 2024, then abolished it in 2025. Same code, same input, two different answers — depending on whether your tzinfo gem is recent enough to know.

If your tzinfo is stale, your app is not using old data. It is following laws that have been repealed.

Truth № 2

Store IANA names.
Keep tzinfo current.

Two halves of the same habit. Store Europe/London, not "London" or "Eastern Time (US & Canada)". Rails names are UI labels — friendly, mapped, sometimes renamed. IANA names are the data model.

And keep tzinfo current, because the database keeps moving. The first habit protects you from churn inside Rails. The second protects you from the world changing under your feet.

For our sunrise feature, this is the moment latitude and longitude stop being enough. The user reads the answer on a clock — and that clock lives in a political zone, not a geographic one.

Lie № 3

Time.now gives current time

Lie number three. Time.now. Of course it gives you the current time. The name says so. The problem is: the current time according to whom? 


            config.time_zone = "UTC"

            Time.now      # => 17:25:55 +0100
            Time.current  # => 16:25:58 UTC

My configuration said UTC. Time.now said no. Time.now reads the system clock and the system zone. It does not consult your application. The fix is one word.

Truth № 3

Time.now asks the machine.
Time.current asks the app.

Time.now reads the operating system. Time.current reads your application. In Rails app code, default to Time.current — you almost always wanted the one that matches the zone you configured.

There are legitimate uses for Time.now: measuring elapsed time, code outside Rails, logging the machine environment. But in everyday app code, you wrote the application — trust it.

Lie № 4

Time.parse is reliable

Lie number four. Time.parse. You give it a string, you get a time. Sounds reasonable. Now look at this string. 


            Time.parse("2026-03-29 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-03-29 09:00:00 +0200

There is no +0200 in the input. The +0200 came from my laptop. If I deploy this code to a server in another zone, it returns a different value for the same string. This is a very common bug and the fix is quite easy as well.

Truth № 4 

            Time.zone = "UTC"

            Time.zone.parse("2026-03-29 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-03-29 09:00:00 UTC

The important part isn't the method name. It's who owns the fallback. Time.parse falls back to whatever the host machine is set to. Time.zone.parse falls back to the zone your application declared. Same kind of assumption — but one you wrote.

Lie № 5

1:30 AM happens once

Lie number five. 1:30 in the morning happens once. Just like every other minute. We are about to find a day where it happens twice. And a day where it does not happen at all.

Daylight saving time was originally an industrial scheduling experiment. It became universal during World War One, then mostly stayed. Most countries that observe it move clocks by one hour, twice a year. And as you may know, each of those moves does something terrible to your code. 


            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("Europe/London")

            tz.local_time(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)

I don't know if you have seen a ghost before, but you're about to see one. I am asking the London zone to give me a Time at 1:30 AM on March 29. 


            tz.local_time(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)
            # => TZInfo::PeriodNotFound

That time never happened in London. The wall clock jumped over it — there is no London instant to return. Store it and you've stored a lie. 


            tz.local_time(2026, 10, 25, 1, 30, 0)
            # => TZInfo::AmbiguousTime

Here is a second one. This one is not really a ghost, it's more of a twin.

When the clocks go back, the same local wall-clock time happens twice. The zone refuses to pick for you — it asks which one you meant. 


            Time.zone = "Europe/London"

            Time.zone.parse("2026-03-29 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 29 Mar 2026 02:30:00 BST +01:00

            Time.zone.parse("2026-10-25 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 01:30:00 BST +01:00

That's not even the worst of it. If you use Time.zone.parse, it will pick one of the two times for you, without raising anything, hoping the right one is picked.

Truth № 5

Nonexistent times need a policy.

Ambiguous times need a choice.

When you take a wall-clock time from a user, use the zone to construct it — tz.local_time in TZInfo, or the equivalent in your library — and handle both errors deliberately.

For a ghost time, you have a policy decision: reject, move forward, or ask. For an ambiguous time, you have a choice: the earlier occurrence, the later one, or asking the user. These are not bugs to swallow with a generic rescue — they are product questions in disguise.

Lie № 6

JSON and the database preserve your time

Lie number six. You did the work in Ruby. You used Time.zone, you handled DST, your tests pass. Now you need to send it somewhere — a JSON response, a database column. This is where you lose what you just built. 


            Time.zone = "UTC"

            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

At this point, you might be feeling safe. You're using zone, the string includes the offset, and the time and date are pretty far from the scary ones we've seen so far. 


            Time.zone = "UTC"

            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

            # => "2026-06-25T08:00:00.000Z"

And yes, nothing broke, nothing raised, you got a valid JSON string for a valid time.

The instant survived. But the original offset did not, and the IANA zone was never there to begin with — +01:00 could have been a dozen zones, and now even +01:00 is gone.

Truth № 6

Outside Ruby objects, don't forget the zone.

Once you leave Ruby for serialization or storage, remember the zone won't follow you. If you need it, make sure to include it.

For our sunrise feature, this is the moment we decide not to store the answer at all. The zone story survives in the database only if we wrote it down ourselves.

Database warning

PostgreSQL: timestamptz

MySQL: datetime

SQLite: no datetime type

If you need to store the time and keep the zone, you'll need to store them separately.

PostgreSQL's timestamptz stores an instant. Not the original offset, not the IANA name — just the moment, normalised to UTC, displayed in the session's zone. The name is misleading; nothing about the zone survives the round-trip.

MySQL has two: datetime stores calendar fields with no zone at all, and timestamp converts through the session zone but only covers a limited range. SQLite doesn't have a datetime type, so you store it as a string or an integer and handle the zone yourself.

If the zone matters, store it next to the instant — IANA name in its own column.

Lie № 7

Noon is when the Sun is highest

Lie number seven, and this one is genuinely cosmic. The clock strikes twelve, you look up, the Sun is at its highest point. Right? That is the definition of noon.

Except it isn't. Not most days.

In London, solar noon happens at:

Feb 11 · 12:14
Nov 3 · 11:44

Same city. Same meridian. On February 11, the Sun reaches its highest point at 12:14. On November 3, it gets there at 11:44. Half an hour apart, depending on which day of the year you ask.

This is the equation of time. The Earth's orbit isn't a circle — it's a slightly squashed ellipse — and the Earth's axis is tilted. Both of those facts mean the Sun runs a bit fast for part of the year and a bit slow for the rest.

The Sun is up to 16 minutes off the clock.

Every year.

The Sun pulls ahead of the clock by up to 16 minutes around November, and falls behind by up to 14 minutes around February. The gap reaches zero a few times a year, but most days the clock and the Sun disagree.

Now, this is probably not something that would bother us programmers but I still wanted to mention it. We use a mean solar time in two manners: to define the length of a day, and to define the hours on the clock.

One year of solar noons.

If you photograph the Sun at the same clock time every day for a year, you don't get a dot. You get this — a figure-8 called an analemma. The vertical axis is the tilt of the Earth. The horizontal axis is the equation of time. Every dot is a noon the clock got wrong.

Truth № 7

The clock is not the Sun.

If you are computing sunrise, sunset, prayer times, the position of any object in the sky — you cannot ask the clock where the Sun is. You have to ask the Sun.

Which is exactly what our sunrise feature does. The clock just labels the answer.

Toolkit

What to take home.

Seven lies is a lot to remember. So here is what to actually take home.

Back to the sunrise.

Remember our one feature. When does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city? We have spent seven lies watching that question break. Now let's actually build it.

Don't 

          User.create!(
            next_sunrise_at: tomorrows_sunrise(user)
          )

Don't store the sunrise. The user moves from Paris to Tokyo, the sunrise is wrong. DST kicks in, the sunrise is wrong. Paraguay abolishes DST, the sunrise is wrong. The user travels to Tromsø in January, the sunrise doesn't exist.

You stored the result of a calculation. You needed to store the inputs that produce it.

Do 

          t.decimal :latitude
          t.decimal :longitude
          t.string  :time_zone

Store the intent: where the user is, and which zone they read time in. Compute the sunrise when you actually need it. A latitude and a longitude don't lie about DST.

Store the question.

Compute the answer.

The mistake isn't computing the sunrise — it's storing the answer instead of storing the question. Latitude, longitude, zone: that's the question. The sunrise is just what the question evaluates to today. 


          Time.use_zone(user.time_zone) do
            Sunrise.compute(
              latitude:  user.latitude,
              longitude: user.longitude,
              date:      Time.zone.tomorrow
            )
          end

This survives DST. It survives the user moving from one country to another. It survives Paraguay. The intent lives in the database, the sunrise is computed on demand. And the answer might be nil — which is fine, we will get to that.

Testing 

          travel_to Time.utc(2026, 10, 25, 0, 30) do
            # A 90,000-second day in London.
          end

Test the evil dates your clock can actually represent. October 25. March 29. February 29. Midnight on the day Paraguay abolished daylight saving.

Leap seconds are a different category — Ruby's Time doesn't model 23:59:60, so travel_to won't help. If your domain needs that precision, isolate the clock source and test it there.

A test that passes on a Tuesday in June proves nothing.

Showing time to humans

10:23 +03:00

10:23 AST

10:23 Asia/Riyadh

10:23 Asia/Riyadh (UTC+3)

Three ways to show a time. The UTC offset is accurate, but most users won't know if "+03:00" has already been applied or not. Acronyms are short, but AST is both Arabia Standard Time and Atlantic Standard Time, and they are four hours apart. IANA names are unambiguous, but unhelpful if you don't recognise the zone.

Combine them. The zone name gives identity, the offset gives arithmetic. Not always relevant, but if your app crosses zones, this is the friendliest format — especially for users who never see DST or never read offsets.

The checklist
Instant? Store a UTC instant
Zone matters? Store the IANA name separately
Current time? Time.current
Calendar math? 1.day, advance, change
Machine input? Require ISO 8601 with an offset
Trusted app-zone string? Time.zone.parse
User wall time? tz.local_time; handle gaps & overlaps
Schedule? Store intent. Compute the instant.
Test? travel_to an evil date

A day is not always 24 hours.

And even the Moon doesn't always rise.

Every line on this slide is something we've already met.

A day is not always 24 hours — that was October in London. Dates don't always follow each other — Rome skipped ten of them in October 1582. Time zones don't sit still — Paraguay abolished daylight saving after my last bundle update.

And then there's the Moon. The Moon's rising drifts about fifty minutes every day, because the Moon orbits Earth and Earth rotates and the two don't synchronise. Some local 24-hour windows just don't contain a rising. The day skips it.

So when somebody asks "when does the Moon rise today?", sometimes the honest answer is: it doesn't. nil isn't a bug — it's the correct return value.

Don't trust your instincts about time. Mine are wrong too.

In a polar winter, the Sun doesn't rise.

For months.

Remember our feature? When does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city? We used Time.zone, we updated tzinfo, we stored IANA names, we computed on demand. We did everything right.

And then somebody opens the app in Tromsø in January. The Sun does not rise. For months.

If your code calls sunrise.strftime somewhere in there, it crashes. Or worse, it quietly shows yesterday's value, and nobody notices until somebody writes to support. Sometimes nil is the only honest answer to a reasonable question.

Time was never going to be simple.
