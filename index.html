For most of human history,
the only clock was the sky.

Before we wrote any of this down, there was one clock: the sky. The Sun told you when to wake up, when to plant, when to harvest. The Moon told you when months were. The stars told you where North was.

Time was something you read, not something you stored.

We invented railways. We invented Ruby.

The sky did not change.

Our code is still trying to catch up.

Then we invented railways, and trains needed schedules, and schedules needed a shared clock. We invented standard time. We invented time zones. We invented Unix timestamps, ISO 8601, Ruby, ActiveSupport.

The sky did not change. The Earth still wobbles, the Moon still slows us down, the year is still not a whole number of days. Our code is still trying to catch up.

Let me show you what trying to catch up looks like.

One feature

When does the Sun rise tomorrow
in your city?

Before we look at any lie, let's pick a feature. One feature. A weather app, a prayer-time app, an astronomy app, doesn't matter. The whole feature is one question: when does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city?

Sounds simple. A time of day, computed from a location. We are going to spend the next forty minutes watching this question get answered wrong, in ten different ways.

Lie № 1

A day is 24 hours

Lie number one. And this one is bold. Everybody knows a day has 24 hours. So 24 times 3600, that's 86,400 seconds. It is the kind of fact you don't even check. You just use it.

Let me show you a morning that does not have 24 hours. 


          Time.zone = "Europe/London"
          t = Time.zone.parse("2026-10-25 00:30:00")
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 00:30:00 BST +01:00

Let's pick a time. Half past midnight in London, the morning of October 25, 2026. Looks innocent. I am going to add a day to it. The simplest thing a developer can do with a time. 


          t + 86400
          # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 23:30:00 GMT +00:00

86,400 seconds. That's a day. Look at the result. Sunday, October 25. We are still on the same day. We added 24 hours and we did not reach Monday.

On October 25, 2026, in London, a day is 90,000 seconds long.

On October 25, 2026, the clocks in the UK go back at 2 AM. The hour between 1 and 2 happens twice. So that day is 25 hours long. 90,000 seconds.

Your scheduling code doesn't know that. + 86400 certainly doesn't know that.

Truth № 1 

          t + 1.day
          # => Mon, 26 Oct 2026 00:30:00 GMT +00:00

1.day is not a number. It is an ActiveSupport::Duration, a value object that knows what it means.

ActiveSupport patches Time#+ to call advance when it sees a Duration, instead of just adding seconds. The fix is one character. And now the math knows about the calendar.

Use the object that means what you want.

This is the rule. The whole talk, in one sentence. 1.day is just the start — it generalises to everything else we are going to see. 


          jan_31 = Time.zone.local(2026, 1, 31)

          jan_31 + 1.month   # => Sat, 28 Feb 2026
          jan_31 + 2.months  # => Tue, 31 Mar 2026
          jan_31 + 30.days   # => Mon, 02 Mar 2026

Three operations, three different answers. + 1.month is end-of-month aware — February 31 does not exist, so it lands on the 28th. + 2.months anchors back to the original day, which is why it lands on March 31, not on the 28th plus a month. And + 30.days just counts days, ignoring every month boundary.

Under the hood, 1.month calls advance — same lesson, different unit. Each one has its own meaning. Pick the one that matches what you actually want.

Same day, different hour


          Time.zone.now.change(
            hour: 9, min: 0, sec: 0
          )

change rewrites parts of a time without touching the rest. Useful when you want "today, but at 9 AM in the user's zone". We are going to see this method again later, in the scheduling example.

Lie № 2

The calendar is stable

You probably trust dates more than times. Dates are simple. A day is a day. No time zones, no leap seconds, no daylight saving. It should be safe.

It is not.

Born December 25, 1642

This is Isaac Newton. We tend to know him for one or two things in physics. His birthday is December 25, 1642. Christmas baby. Easy to remember.

Born January 4, 1643

Both are correct. Britain switched calendars in 1752 and just deleted eleven days. So the same person ends up with two birthdays, depending on which country's calendar you ask. 


          Date.new(1642, 12, 25, Date::ENGLAND) ==
            Date.new(1643, 1, 4)
          # => true

Ruby's Date class knows about this. By default, dates after October 15, 1582 are Gregorian, and dates before October 4, 1582 are Julian. But you can pass a third argument: Date::ENGLAND, Date::ITALY, Date::GREGORIAN, Date::JULIAN.

Ruby is only partially proleptic — it actually knows which countries switched when. Most other libraries do not.

Truth № 2

If the date is older than your grandparents,
ask which calendar.

Most apps will never hit this. But if you ever deal with historical data — genealogy, archives, history apps — you have to choose.

And it gets worse. Some libraries are proleptic Gregorian, some are not, and they will disagree silently.

Lie № 3

Your zone data is current

Calendars changed centuries ago. Zone rules keep changing in our lifetime. Same problem, just faster. 


      require "tzinfo"

      zone = "America/Asuncion"
      tz   = TZInfo::Timezone.get(zone)
      utc  = Time.utc(2025, 7, 1, 12, 0, 0)
      local = tz.utc_to_local(utc)

      puts "UTC time  : #{utc}"
      puts "Local time: #{local}"

      # UTC time  : 2025-07-01 12:00:00 UTC
      # Local time: 2025-07-01 08:00:00

Let's look at Paraguay. If you run this code on an old app that hasn't been updated for a while, it will give you a local time of 8 AM. 


      require "tzinfo"

      zone = "America/Asuncion"
      tz   = TZInfo::Timezone.get(zone)
      utc  = Time.utc(2025, 7, 1, 12, 0, 0)
      local = tz.utc_to_local(utc)

      puts "UTC time  : #{utc}"
      puts "Local time: #{local}"

      # UTC time  : 2025-07-01 12:00:00 UTC
      # Local time: 2025-07-01 09:00:00

Then, run the same on a recent version of Rails and you will see the same code produces a different time.

That's because Paraguay observed DST until late 2024, and then abolished it in 2025.

If your bundle is stale, your application is doing the right thing for a country that no longer exists.

Truth № 3 

            $ bundle update tzinfo

The fix is to update tzinfo. That's it. The hard part is not the command, it's remembering this gem exists. It is one of the few gems that needs to be updated not because of new features, but because the world keeps changing under it.

Lie № 4

Time zones are geography

Lie number four. Time zones look like geography. You take a globe, you draw 24 vertical lines, you call each strip a zone. That is the version we teach children. The real one is messier.

Before 1840, every town set its clock by the Sun.

Penzance · 11:38
Bristol · 11:51
London · 12:00
Norwich · 12:05

Before 1840, every town set its own clock by the Sun. Noon was when the Sun was directly overhead, and the Sun is in a slightly different place over Penzance than over Norwich. So when London read 12:00, Penzance read 11:38. Bristol read 11:51. Norwich read 12:05.

These were not approximations. These were the times. Local mean solar time. And it was fine — because nobody could move faster than the Sun did.

Then we invented trains.

1847 · one time, one country.

Then we invented trains. A two-hour journey from London to Bristol now meant arriving in another time. Railway timetables became unreadable. Stations posted two clocks, and Bristol cathedral kept two minute hands — one for Bristol time, one for railway time.

In 1847, British railways agreed to use Greenwich Mean Time at every station, regardless of where it sat. Local solar time died for everyone except Greenwich. Other countries followed.

Then politics drew the lines.

Then governments started bending the lines. China is one zone wide enough to be five. India is offset by half an hour. Nepal by forty-five minutes. Spain sits on the same meridian as Britain but reads its clocks an hour ahead, because Franco aligned with Berlin in 1940 and nobody ever moved it back.

Time zones look like geography. They are political maps with a sundial pinned to them.

We traded astronomical accuracy
for human convenience.

That's the deal. The Sun stopped telling us the time. The trains did. Then the politicians did. We agreed it was worth it, because the alternative was unreadable timetables and meetings that started at different moments for different attendees.

The receipt for that trade is a database.

The IANA database is the receipt.

600+ zones · updated several times a year

The IANA time zone database. More than 600 zones. Updated several times a year — because somewhere in the world, a government has just decided to do something different with their clocks.

Remember Lie 3 — bundle update tzinfo? That's not a chore. That's you collecting the latest receipt.

Truth № 4

Store IANA names.
Avoid Rails names.

If you need more context than just a point in time, you might want to store not only the time but also the time zones.

In this case, you should store the IANA name of the time zone, not the Rails name, because the Rails name is not stable. It can be changed by the Rails team, and it has been changed in the past. The IANA name is stable, and it's the one that is used by the tzinfo gem, which is the one that Rails uses under the hood.

Lie № 5

Time.now gives current time

Lie number five. Time.now. Of course it gives you the current time. The name says so. The problem is: the current time according to whom? 


            config.time_zone = "UTC"

            Time.now      # => 17:25:55 +0100
            Time.current  # => 16:25:58 UTC

My configuration said UTC. Time.now said no. Time.now reads the system clock and the system zone. It does not consult your application. The fix is one word.

Truth № 5

Time.current
Always.

Time.current. Always. Not "when convenient", not "when you remember". Always. Time.now reads the operating system. Time.current reads your application. You wrote the application. Trust it.

Lie № 6

Time.parse is reliable

Lie number six. Time.parse. You give it a string, you get a time. Sounds reasonable. Now look at this string. 


            Time.parse("2026-03-29 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-03-29 09:00:00 +0200

There is no +0200 in the input. The +0200 came from my laptop. If I deploy this code to a server in another zone, it returns a different value for the same string. This is a very common bug and the fix is quite easy as well.

Truth № 6 

            Time.zone.parse("2026-03-29 09:00:00")
            # => 2026-03-29 09:00:00 UTC

Five characters and your code won't assume anything on your behalf.

Lie № 7

1:30 AM happens once

Lie number seven. 1:30 in the morning happens once. Just like every other minute. We are about to find a day where it happens twice. And a day where it does not happen at all.

Daylight saving time was originally an industrial scheduling experiment. It became universal during World War One, then mostly stayed. Most countries that observe it move clocks by one hour, twice a year. And as you may know, each of those moves does something terrible to your code. 


            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("Europe/London")

            ghost = Time.new(2026, 3, 29, 1, 30, 0)

I don't know if you have seen a ghost before, but you're about to see one. 


            tz.local_to_utc(ghost)

That time never happened. Anywhere. To anyone. Store it and you've stored a lie. 


            # => TZInfo::PeriodNotFound

And actually the code doesn't even work, you'll get a TZInfo::PeriodNotFound error. 


            tz = TZInfo::Timezone.get("Europe/London")
            ghost = Time.new(2026, 10, 25, 1, 30, 0)

            tz.local_to_utc(ghost)
            # => TZInfo::AmbiguousTime

Here is a second one. This one is not really a ghost, it's more of a twin.

When the clocks go back, the same local time happens twice. If you try to convert it to UTC, you get an error because it's ambiguous. You have to choose which one you mean. 


            Time.zone.parse("2026-03-29 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 29 Mar 2026 02:30:00 BST +01:00

            Time.zone.parse("2026-10-25 01:30:00")
            # => Sun, 25 Oct 2026 01:30:00 BST +01:00

That's not even the worst of it. If you use Time.zone.parse, it will pick one of the two times for you, without raising anything, hoping the right one is picked.

Truth № 7

When parsing user input,
use TZInfo::Timezone#local_to_utc
and rescue both errors.

The fix to this problem is to use the timezone's local_to_utc method, which will raise an error if the time is invalid or ambiguous.

Okay, I know this is quite unlikely to happen, but keep in mind the time zone rules are changing all the time, you can never know what weird edge case your app will need to support in the future.

Lie № 8

JSON and the database preserve your time

Lie number eight. You did the work in Ruby. You used Time.zone, you handled DST, your tests pass. Now you need to send it somewhere — a JSON response, a database column. This is where you lose what you just built. 


            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

At this point, you might be feeling safe. You're using zone, the string includes the offset, and the time and date are pretty far from the scary ones we've seen so far. 


            str = "2026-06-25 09:00:00+01:00"
            Time.zone.parse(str).to_json

            # => "2026-06-25T08:00:00.000Z"

And yes, nothing broke, nothing raised, you got a valid JSON string for a valid time.

But you lost something important on the way: the offset. The time is now in UTC, and you have no way to know it was originally in BST.

Truth № 8

Outside Ruby objects, don't forget the zone.

Once you leave Ruby for serialization or storage, remember the zone won't follow you. If you need it, make sure to include it.

Database warning

PostgreSQL: timestamptz

MySQL: datetime

SQLite: no datetime type

If you need to store the time and keep the zone, you'll need to store them separately.

PostgreSQL has a timestamptz type that stores the time in UTC and the offset, but it doesn't store the IANA name of the time zone.

MySQL's datetime type doesn't store the time zone at all.

SQLite doesn't have a datetime type, so you have to store it as a string or an integer, and you have to handle the time zone yourself.

Lie № 9

A minute is 60 seconds

Lie number nine. A minute has 60 seconds. The very first slide of this talk showed you a minute that had 61. We are coming back to it.

Earth's rotation is slowing down. The Moon pulls on our oceans, the friction acts like a brake, and over centuries the day gets longer. But do you know how it impacts you?

TAI
International Atomic Time

Today, the definition of the second is not based on the Earth's rotation anymore, but on the vibrations of a cesium atom. This is called TAI, or International Atomic Time, and it's a continuous time scale.

UTC
Coordinated Universal Time

But you and me, we use UTC, which is a time scale that is based on TAI but has leap seconds added to keep it in sync with the Earth's rotation.

Yes, you might have heard about leap seconds, but they exist. And we add or subtract them as needed, which is usually every few years. The last one was added in December 2016, and none have been added since.

TAI − UTC = 37 seconds

As of today, TAI is 37 seconds ahead of UTC.

This means, there are some cases where a minute is not 60 seconds.

UTC TAI UNIX timestamp
2016-12-31 23:59:59 2017-01-01 00:00:35 1483228799
2016-12-31 23:59:60 2017-01-01 00:00:36 1483228799
2017-01-01 00:00:00 2017-01-01 00:00:37 1483228800

There's a lot to say there. First, as we said, TAI is continuous, so it doesn't have a leap second.

UTC has a leap second, so it has a 23:59:60. This is a valid time in UTC.

UNIX timestamps count seconds since 1970-01-01 UTC but ignore leap seconds, so they don't represent them. This means that the same UNIX timestamp can correspond to two different UTC times, which is a problem if you're using UNIX timestamps to store time.

Lie № 10

Noon is when the Sun is highest

Lie number ten, and this one is genuinely cosmic. The clock strikes twelve, you look up, the Sun is at its highest point. Right? That is the definition of noon.

Except it isn't. Not most days.

In London, solar noon happens at:

Feb 11 · 12:14
Nov 3 · 11:44

Same city. Same meridian. On February 11, the Sun reaches its highest point at 12:14. On November 3, it gets there at 11:44. Half an hour apart, depending on which day of the year you ask.

This is the equation of time. The Earth's orbit isn't a circle — it's a slightly squashed ellipse — and the Earth's axis is tilted. Both of those facts mean the Sun runs a bit fast for part of the year and a bit slow for the rest.

The Sun is up to 16 minutes off the clock.

Twice a year.

The Sun pulls ahead of the clock by up to 16 minutes around November, and falls behind by up to 14 minutes around February. Twice a year, the gap closes to zero. The rest of the time, the Sun is somewhere else.

One year of solar noons, photographed.

If you photograph the Sun at the same clock time every day for a year, you don't get a dot. You get this — a figure-8 called an analemma. The vertical axis is the tilt of the Earth. The horizontal axis is the equation of time. Every dot is a noon the clock got wrong.

Truth № 10

The clock is not the Sun.

If you are computing sunrise, sunset, prayer times, the position of any object in the sky — you cannot ask the clock where the Sun is. You have to ask the Sun.

Which is exactly what our sunrise feature does. The clock just labels the answer.

Toolkit

What to take home.

Ten lies is a lot to remember. So here is what to actually take home.

Back to the sunrise.

Remember our one feature. When does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city? We have spent ten lies watching that question break. Now let's actually build it.

Don't 

          User.create!(
            next_sunrise_at: tomorrows_sunrise(user)
          )

Don't store the sunrise. The user moves from Paris to Tokyo, the sunrise is wrong. DST kicks in, the sunrise is wrong. Paraguay abolishes DST, the sunrise is wrong. The user travels to Tromsø in January, the sunrise doesn't exist.

You stored the result of a calculation. You needed to store the inputs that produce it.

Do 

          t.decimal :latitude
          t.decimal :longitude
          t.string  :time_zone

Store the intent: where the user is, and which zone they read time in. Compute the sunrise when you actually need it. A latitude and a longitude don't lie about DST. 


          Time.use_zone(user.time_zone) do
            observer = Observer.new(
              latitude:  Angle.from_degrees(user.latitude),
              longitude: Angle.from_degrees(user.longitude),
            )
            calculator = RiseTransitSetCalculator.new(
              body:     Sun,
              observer: observer
            )
            calculator.event_on(Date.tomorrow).rising_time
          end

This survives DST. It survives the user moving from one country to another. It survives Paraguay. The intent lives in the database, the sunrise is computed on demand. And the answer might be nil — which is fine, we will get to that.

Testing 

          travel_to Time.utc(2026, 10, 25, 0, 30) do
            # A 90,000-second day in London.
          end

Test the evil dates. October 25. March 29. February 29. December 31 at 23:59:60. Midnight on the day Paraguay abolished daylight saving.

A test that passes on a Tuesday in June proves nothing.

Showing time to humans

10:23 +03:00

10:23 AST

10:23 Asia/Riyadh

Three ways to show a time. The UTC offset is accurate, but most users won't know if "+03:00" has already been applied or not. Acronyms are short, but AST is both Arabia Standard Time and Atlantic Standard Time, and they are four hours apart. IANA names are unambiguous, but unhelpful if you don't recognise the zone.

10:23 Asia/Riyadh (UTC+3)

Combine them. The zone name gives you identity, the offset gives you arithmetic. Not always relevant, but if your app crosses zones, this is the friendliest format for users — especially the ones who never see DST or never read offsets.

The checklist
Store? timestamptz + IANA zone column
Parse? Time.zone.parse
Math? 1.day, change, advance
Schedule? Store intent. Compute the instant.
Test? travel_to an evil date

2026-01-09, Paris

The Moon does not set.

Not late. Not early. It just doesn't. Some days, in some places, the Moon does not set at all.

So when somebody asks "when does the Moon set today?", sometimes the correct answer is: it doesn't. nil can be a valid answer to a reasonable question.

In a polar winter, the Sun doesn't rise.

For months.

Remember our feature? When does the Sun rise tomorrow in your city? We used Time.zone, we updated tzinfo, we stored IANA names, we computed on demand. We did everything right.

And then somebody opens the app in Tromsø in January. The Sun does not rise. For months.

If your code calls sunrise.strftime somewhere in there, it crashes. Or worse, it quietly shows yesterday's value, and nobody notices until somebody writes to support. Sometimes nil is the only honest answer to a reasonable question.

On the ISS, they use UTC.

They cross 16 time zones a day.

On the International Space Station, they live by UTC. There is no real alternative. They orbit the planet every 90 minutes, which means they pass through sixteen time zones every day.

If they used local time, they would change zones every five minutes.

On the Moon, NASA is still arguing about what time it is.

This is not a joke. NASA is actively working on a standard called Coordinated Lunar Time. Clocks tick about 58 microseconds per day faster on the Moon than on Earth. Sounds like nothing. It is enough to ruin GPS-grade navigation.

On Mars, a day is 24 hours and 39 minutes.

If we ever live there, Martians are going to need leap something. Maybe leap minutes. Maybe a whole new calendar.

Some Ruby developer in fifty years is going to open a bug ticket about this. Hopefully I will be retired.

Time was never going to be simple.

We started with a calendar that lost ten days in 1582. We ended with the Moon, where they have not yet decided what a second is.

Four hundred and forty years, and the only thing we have agreed on is that we are not done arguing.
